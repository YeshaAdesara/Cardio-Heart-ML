Understanding Heart Disease

Heart disease is the leading cause of death worldwide. Learning about it is the first step to prevention.

What is Heart Disease?

Heart disease refers to any condition affecting the heart. There are many types, some of which are preventable. Unlike cardiovascular disease, which includes problems with the entire circulatory system, heart disease affects only the heart.

According to the CDC, heart disease is the leading cause of death in the United States. Around 1 in 4 deaths occur due to heart disease, affecting all genders, races, and ethnic groups.

Types of Heart Disease

Coronary Artery Disease

The most common type. It develops when arteries supplying blood to the heart become clogged with plaque, causing them to harden and narrow. This reduces blood supply, and the heart receives less oxygen. Over time, the heart muscle weakens, risking heart failure and arrhythmias.

Congenital Heart Defects

A person with a congenital heart defect is born with a heart problem. Types include:

  • Abnormal heart valves: Valves may not open properly or may leak blood
  • Septal defects: A hole in the wall between heart chambers
  • Atresia: One of the heart valves is missing

Arrhythmia

Irregular heartbeat when electrical impulses don't work properly. Types include:

  • Tachycardia: Rapid heartbeat
  • Bradycardia: Slow heartbeat
  • Premature contractions: Early heartbeat
  • Atrial fibrillation: Irregular heartbeat

Dilated Cardiomyopathy

The heart chambers become dilated, meaning the heart muscle stretches and becomes thinner. The heart becomes weaker and cannot pump blood properly. Usually affects people aged 20-60.

Myocardial Infarction (Heart Attack)

An interruption of blood flow to the heart that can damage or destroy part of the heart muscle. Most commonly caused by plaque, blood clots, or arterial spasms.

Heart Failure

The heart is still working but not as well as it should. Can result from untreated coronary artery disease, high blood pressure, arrhythmias, and other conditions.

Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy

Usually develops from a genetic problem. The walls of the muscle thicken, making contractions harder. It is the main cause of cardiac death among athletes and people under 35.

Symptoms

Blood Vessel Disease Symptoms

  • Chest pain, tightness, pressure and discomfort (angina)
  • Shortness of breath
  • Pain, numbness, weakness or coldness in legs or arms
  • Pain in the neck, jaw, throat, upper abdomen or back

Arrhythmia Symptoms

  • Fluttering in your chest
  • Racing or slow heartbeat
  • Chest pain or discomfort
  • Lightheadedness, dizziness, fainting

Cardiomyopathy Symptoms

  • Breathlessness with activity or at rest
  • Swelling of legs, ankles and feet
  • Fatigue and irregular heartbeats
  • Dizziness, lightheadedness and fainting

Heart Infection Symptoms

  • Fever and shortness of breath
  • Weakness, fatigue, swelling
  • Changes in heart rhythm
  • Dry or persistent cough, skin rashes
Seek emergency medical care if you experience: Chest pain, Shortness of breath, or Fainting.

Prevention

While some heart diseases can't be prevented, lifestyle changes can significantly reduce your risk:

Don't smoke
Control blood pressure, cholesterol, and diabetes
Exercise at least 30 minutes daily
Eat a diet low in salt and saturated fat
Maintain a healthy weight
Reduce and manage stress
Practice good hygiene
