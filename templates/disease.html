Understanding Heart Disease

Heart disease remains the leading cause of death globally. Knowledge is the first and most powerful step toward prevention and early detection.

What is Heart Disease?

Heart disease refers to a range of conditions that affect the heart's structure and function. Unlike cardiovascular disease, which encompasses the entire circulatory system, heart disease specifically targets the heart organ itself.

According to the CDC, heart disease is the leading cause of death in the United States, responsible for approximately 1 in every 4 deaths. It affects people of all ages, genders, races, and ethnic backgrounds.

The good news is that many forms of heart disease can be prevented or managed through healthy lifestyle choices, early detection, and proper medical care.

Types of Heart Disease

Coronary Artery Disease (CAD)

The most prevalent form of heart disease. It develops when the coronary arteries supplying blood to the heart become narrowed or blocked by plaque buildup (atherosclerosis). This reduces blood flow and oxygen supply to the heart muscle, potentially leading to chest pain (angina), heart attacks, heart failure, or arrhythmias over time.

Congenital Heart Defects

Structural heart problems present from birth. These defects form during fetal development and vary greatly in severity. Common types include:

  • Abnormal heart valves: Valves may not open or close properly, causing blood to leak backward
  • Septal defects: Holes in the walls separating heart chambers, allowing blood to mix abnormally
  • Atresia: Complete absence of one or more heart valves, severely affecting blood flow

Arrhythmia (Irregular Heartbeat)

Conditions where the heart's electrical system malfunctions, causing irregular heartbeat patterns. Types include:

  • Tachycardia: Abnormally rapid heartbeat (over 100 bpm at rest)
  • Bradycardia: Abnormally slow heartbeat (under 60 bpm)
  • Premature contractions: Extra, early heartbeats that disrupt normal rhythm
  • Atrial fibrillation: Rapid, chaotic electrical signals causing the upper chambers to quiver

Dilated Cardiomyopathy

A condition where the heart chambers become enlarged and the muscle walls thin out, weakening the heart's ability to pump blood effectively. It most commonly affects adults aged 20-60 and can result from various causes including genetics, infections, and toxin exposure.

Myocardial Infarction (Heart Attack)

Occurs when blood flow to a portion of the heart muscle is suddenly blocked, causing tissue damage or death. Most commonly triggered by plaque rupture, blood clots, or coronary artery spasms. Requires immediate emergency medical treatment to minimize permanent damage.

Heart Failure

A chronic condition where the heart cannot pump blood efficiently enough to meet the body's needs. The heart is still beating, but not with sufficient force. Can result from untreated coronary artery disease, chronic high blood pressure, arrhythmias, and other cardiovascular conditions.

Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy

A genetic condition where the heart muscle walls become abnormally thick, making it harder for the heart to pump blood. It is a leading cause of sudden cardiac death among young athletes and people under 35, often with no prior symptoms.

Signs & Symptoms

Blood Vessel Disease Symptoms

  • Chest pain, tightness, pressure and discomfort (angina)
  • Shortness of breath during activity or at rest
  • Pain, numbness, weakness or coldness in legs or arms
  • Pain in the neck, jaw, throat, upper abdomen or back

Arrhythmia Symptoms

  • Fluttering sensation in the chest (palpitations)
  • Racing heartbeat (tachycardia) or slow heartbeat (bradycardia)
  • Chest pain, pressure or discomfort
  • Lightheadedness, dizziness or fainting spells

Cardiomyopathy Symptoms

  • Breathlessness with physical activity or even at rest
  • Swelling of the legs, ankles and feet (edema)
  • Persistent fatigue and irregular heartbeats
  • Dizziness, lightheadedness and fainting episodes

Heart Infection Symptoms

  • Fever, chills and shortness of breath
  • General weakness, fatigue and body swelling
  • Changes in normal heart rhythm patterns
  • Dry or persistent cough, unusual skin rashes or spots
Seek emergency medical care immediately if you experience: severe chest pain, sudden shortness of breath, or unexplained fainting.

Prevention Strategies

While some heart disease risk factors (age, genetics) cannot be changed, many can be significantly reduced through proactive lifestyle modifications:

Quit smoking and avoid secondhand smoke
Monitor and control blood pressure regularly
Exercise at least 30 minutes most days
Follow a heart-healthy diet (low salt, low saturated fat)
Maintain a healthy body weight (BMI 18.5-24.9)
Manage stress through meditation or relaxation
Get regular health screenings and check-ups
Manage diabetes and cholesterol levels effectively
